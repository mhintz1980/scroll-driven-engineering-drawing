# Product

## Register

brand

## Product

Mark Hintz's manufacturing-first portfolio, centered on a standalone spatial drawing-package route.

The current flagship experience is `/drawing-package`: a paper-desk projection diorama where a real engineering drawing sits on a dim drafting desk, the viewport acts as an inspection camera, and active project details project upward from the 2D drawing into crisp media and metadata.

## Users

**Phase 1, now:** Freelance clients. Engineering managers, manufacturing operations leads, procurement decision-makers, precision machining shops, industrial OEMs, and pump/power equipment companies. They are evaluating whether Mark has the fabrication credibility and technical depth to handle mechanical design, DFM review, documentation, internal tooling, or manufacturing-support work.

**Phase 2, later:** Long-term employment. Recruiters and hiring managers at mid-size manufacturers scanning for a mechanical designer who can also build useful software tools without needing a separate dev team.

Both audiences have seen generic CAD portfolios and generic developer portfolios. This site must prove that Mark has touched parts, worked around machines, understood inspection constraints, and can turn that manufacturing knowledge into systems.

## Product Purpose

The portfolio is a credibility engine. A qualified visitor should leave knowing:

1. Mark designs parts, fixtures, and systems that survive fabrication, assembly, inspection, and field use.
2. His drawings, GD&T, and documentation are built with manufacturability, inspection, and tool life in mind.
3. His software and AI work supports manufacturing operations; it is not a pivot away from mechanical work.

Success is a qualified lead reaching out for freelance work, DFM support, documentation help, internal tooling, or a relevant mechanical/software hybrid role.

## Brand Personality

Blunt, competent, floor-tested.

The voice is direct because the experience is real. Shop-floor specificity beats marketing abstraction. Use the language of someone who understands tolerances, inspection, workholding, fitment, documentation, field constraints, and the cost of vague drawings.

Not polished-corporate. Not startup-cute. Not "innovation thought leader." Not a software-first developer portfolio wearing a CAD costume.

## Experience Direction

The current visual north star is **paper desk projection diorama**.

The page should feel like a precision drawing pulled onto a drafting desk in a dim office, then turned into an inspection rig. The drawing is physical paper, not a black void. Active station details project out of the 2D drawing into a lifted detail object with crisp media, a visible projection beam, a paper-origin ring, and a technical metadata plate.

The experience should feel cinematic and spatial, but still mechanically grounded. It should look like a credible shop/engineering artifact being inspected, not a sci-fi dashboard.

## Anti-References

- Plain black background with white CAD lines and floating HUD labels.
- Pixelated detail text or media caused by magnifying tiny substrate-space UI.
- Generic Wix/Squarespace developer portfolios with gradients, glassmorphism, and "I'm passionate about building things" copy.
- SaaS-cream landing pages: beige sections, pill buttons, rounded-everything, generic icon grids, and three-column feature cards.
- Overly techy neon developer portfolios that signal software-first identity and treat mechanical work as a prop.
- Any portfolio that leads with frameworks and languages instead of what was built, fabricated, inspected, documented, or improved.
- Dribbble-bait: beautiful motion that says nothing specific.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the work, not the wrapper.** Real project photos, real drawings, real constraints, real outcomes. The design exists to frame fabrication credibility.
2. **Specificity is the brand.** "Sub-thou tolerances" beats "precision engineering." "Sin blocks and Micro Height" beats "advanced inspection." Every generic phrase loses proof.
3. **Manufacturing-first hierarchy.** Mechanical design and fabrication experience are the primary identity. Software and AI tools are supporting proof that manufacturing knowledge scales.
4. **Projection, not HUD.** Active details should feel projected from paper into space. Readable station content belongs in crisp screen-space, not inside the zoomed substrate.
5. **Physical before digital.** Paper texture, desk lighting, drawing ink, shadows, and projection light should carry the route before abstract UI decoration does.
6. **Earned confidence, not performed confidence.** Let the work, constraints, and outcomes carry the authority. No hype language.
7. **Floor-ready, not portfolio-pretty.** The aesthetic should feel at home in a precision shop's front office or engineering review room, not a design agency gallery.

## Content Priorities

- Mechanical projects are the primary signal.
- Station media should show parts, drawings, renderings, fixtures, packages, or technical visual proof.
- Software and AI should be framed as operational leverage for manufacturing workflows.
- Copy should prioritize concrete nouns, tolerances, materials, operations, inspection methods, constraints, and outcomes.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Ensure sufficient contrast on all text, especially over projection overlays and paper textures. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Avoid seizure-inducing animation. Alt text on project imagery should describe the technical content, not marketing mood.
